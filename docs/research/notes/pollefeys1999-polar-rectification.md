# Setting

**Problem class:** rectifying a *weakly calibrated* (uncalibrated) stereo image pair — only the fundamental matrix is available, no metric calibration or known projection matrices — for **arbitrary camera motion**, explicitly including the case where the epipole of either image lies **inside** that image (general/forward motion). This is precisely the case classical planar-homography rectification cannot represent at all: mapping an in-image epipole to the vanishing point of the epipolar pencil under a 2-D homography would require an unboundedly large output image (Abstract; §1, PDF p.1: "This approach fails when the epipoles are located in the images since this would have to result in infinitely large images").

**Inputs:**
- The **oriented** fundamental matrix $F$ between the two views — "The only required information is the oriented fundamental matrix" (Abstract, PDF p.1). Ordinary (unoriented) $F$ only fixes each epipolar line up to a non-zero scale factor (i.e. as an undirected line); *orienting* it restricts the ambiguity to a **strictly positive** scale factor (§2, PDF p.2), which is what lets the method consistently split every epipolar line into a positive and negative half and match "positive half of line in image 1" to "positive half of line in image 2" — the operation the whole row-by-row construction depends on.
- Obtaining the orientation requires **one additional point correspondence** beyond the $\geq 7$ point matches already needed to estimate $F$ itself (§2, PDF p.2: "Besides the epipolar geometry one point match is needed (note that 7 or more matches were needed anyway to determine the epipolar geometry)").
- No projection matrices, no intrinsics/extrinsics, no metric information of any kind is required — $F$ (oriented) is sufficient. If projection matrices *do* happen to be available, an alternative homography formula is given (Eq. 4, PDF p.2) but this is optional, not required.

**Outputs:**
- A pair of rectified images built by **direct, nonlinear resampling of the originals** — "The whole rectification process is carried out directly in the images" (Abstract, PDF p.1) — with corresponding points on corresponding rows, no 3-D operations of any kind (contrast with Roy et al.'s cylindrical method below, which "performs all operations in 3-dimensional space").
- **Guaranteed no pixel loss:** "A minimal image size without any pixel loss is guaranteed" (Abstract, PDF p.1) — no region of either source image is compressed away.
- **Bounded output size, derived purely from the source image dimensions** $W\times H$ (§3 "Constructing the rectified image", PDF p.4): "The height is bound by the contour of the image $2\times(W+H)$. The width is bound by the diagonal $\sqrt{W^2+H^2}$." The paper further asserts (without a cited formal proof) that this size is the *minimum* achievable without pixel compression: "the image size is uniquely determined with our procedure and that it is the minimum that can be achieved without pixel compression" (PDF p.4).

# Core idea

Reparametrize each image in **polar coordinates centered on its own epipole** (§3, PDF p.3: "The key idea of our new rectification method consists of reparameterizing the image with polar coordinates (around the epipoles)"). Because orientation reduces the matching ambiguity to *half* epipolar lines, only the positive longitudinal (radial) coordinate needs to be swept for each angular sector — the corresponding half epipolar lines are obtained from the transfer equation $l' \sim H^{-\top}l$ (Eq. 2, PDF p.2) with orientation applied. Crucially, this reparametrization never maps the epipole to infinity — the epipole is simply the pole of the polar system, sitting inside the coordinate frame rather than being pushed outward — which is exactly why the method can represent the in-image-epipole case that a mapping-to-infinity (planar homography) construction cannot.

The rectified image is built **row by row**: each output row is one angular sector / half epipolar line, and the radial (longitudinal) distance along that line is preserved unchanged — no resampling occurs along the ray itself, only across rays (§3 "Constructing the rectified image", PDF p.4; Fig. 6 depicts the $(x,y)\to(r,\theta)$ transform with a non-uniform $\theta$-axis). Before any rows are built, the **common angular range** shared by both images must be found: for each image separately, the extreme epipolar lines (the ones touching the image's own corners) are located by classifying the epipole's position into one of **9 regions** relative to the image rectangle (Fig. 3, PDF p.3; corners labelled $a,b,c,d$) — e.g. "if the epipole $e$ is in region 1 the area between $eb$ and $ed$" bounds the extreme lines for that image (§3 "Determining the common region", PDF p.3). The second image's extreme lines are then transferred into the first image (via the same homography $H$) and intersected with the first image's own extreme lines to yield the common region (Fig. 4, PDF p.3).

Within that common range, the **angular step $\Delta\theta_i$ between consecutive rows is chosen independently per half epipolar line**, not uniformly: "To avoid losing pixel information the area of every pixel should be at least preserved when transformed to the rectified image. The worst case pixel is always located on the image border opposite the epipole" (§3 "Determining the distance between epipolar lines", PDF p.3). Figure 5 (PDF p.3) gives the geometric construction: using the congruence of triangles $abc$ and $a'b'c'$, the minimal step that keeps the distance $|a'c'|$ at least one pixel is obtained by moving $\lvert bc\rvert/\lvert ac\rvert$ pixels from the previous border point. **The same computation is repeated independently in the other image, the resulting candidate epipolar line is transferred back into the first image, and the smaller of the two candidate steps is the one actually used**: "The same procedure can be carried out in the other image. In this case the obtained epipolar line should be transferred back to the first image. **The minimum of both displacements is carried out**" (§3, PDF p.4, emphasis mine) — this per-line, min-of-both-images rule is what prevents pixel loss on whichever of the two images happens to have the coarser local angular sampling at that particular line, without inflating the output beyond what either image alone would need.

If the epipole itself lies inside the image, an arbitrary starting epipolar line is chosen and the angular sweep is extended past $360°$, overlapping by the size of the stereo-matching window, specifically to avoid a seam/boundary artifact where the sweep wraps around: "If the epipole is in the image an arbitrary epipolar line can be chosen as starting point. In this case boundary effects can be avoided by adding an overlap of the size of the matching window of the stereo algorithm (i.e. use more than 360 degrees)" (§3, PDF p.3).

Per output row, the epipolar line's endpoint coordinates (distance of the first and last pixel) are stored in a lookup table, giving a simple inverse mapping back to the original image, with interpolation available for sub-pixel accuracy (§3 "Transfering information back", PDF p.4). For warping a *whole* image back efficiently — rather than looking up the table once per output pixel — the paper recommends filling pixels radially, all pixels between two consecutive epipolar lines at once ("The image can be reconstructed radially (i.e. radar like)"), citing Wolberg's *Digital Image Warping* [17] for general background (§3, PDF p.4).

# Assumptions

1. **Oriented fundamental matrix available** (hard): the method needs $F$ plus its orientation (sign convention), the latter obtained from one point correspondence beyond the $\geq 7$ used to estimate $F$'s entries (Abstract; §2, PDF p.2). Without a correctly signed $F$, the positive/negative-half convention used throughout §3 is undefined.
2. **The seed point match used for orientation is itself a correct correspondence** (hard, for the orientation step specifically): the sign of $H$ is fixed by requiring $f_l(m)$ and $f_{l'}(m')$ to carry the same sign for one known matching pair $(m,m')$ (Eq. 5, §2, PDF p.2); the paper does not discuss what happens if this seed pair is itself mismatched, but by construction a wrong seed match would flip the positive/negative convention for the entire image (`?` — not discussed in the extracted text).
3. **Standard two-view projective/pinhole epipolar geometry** (hard, implicit): every geometric construction in §3 — straight epipolar lines converging at a single epipole per image, the 9-region corner classification (Fig. 3), the congruent-triangle distance argument (Fig. 5) — presumes the ordinary two-view model in which epipolar lines are straight lines through the epipole. The paper never states this as a numbered assumption; it is foundational to the method rather than an explicit precondition (`?`).
4. **A non-empty common angular range exists between the two images** (soft/implicit): the "determining the common region" step (Fig. 4, PDF p.3) intersects each image's own extreme-epipolar-line range; the paper does not discuss the degenerate case where this intersection is empty, i.e. the two images share no common epipolar-line range at all (`?`).

# Failure regime

The paper does not present this as a method with sharp failure boundaries — the whole point of the design (per the Introduction's contrast with the traditional and cylindrical methods) is to handle *all* camera geometries without the output blowing up or becoming impossible, unlike planar rectification. What the paper *does* flag as inherent limits:

- **The epipole is a genuine singularity for depth estimation, not fixed by rectification:** in the desk/volleyball experiment (epipole inside the image, §4, PDF p.5) the paper states "there is an important depth uncertainty around the epipole. In fact the epipole forms a singularity for the depth estimation. In the depth map of Figure 12 an artefact can be seen around the position of the epipole. The extend is much longer in one specific direction due to the matching ambiguity in this direction." This is a property of stereo triangulation near the epipole itself (any rectification scheme inherits it), not a defect specific to the polar construction — the paper is explicit that the rectification "works properly" here (PDF p.4: "the methods works properly when the epipole is in the image"); the singularity shows up downstream in disparity/depth estimation.
- **No formal correctness/minimality proof is given or cited** — the size-minimality claim ("the minimum that can be achieved without pixel compression", PDF p.4) is asserted from the construction, not proven with a cited reference (contrast with Fusiello et al. 2000, who *do* defer their construction's correctness proof to a companion technical report).
- **Output appearance is non-rectangular and visibly distorted** compared to the standard homography-rectified pair — visible directly in Fig. 8's side-by-side comparison (standard method top, new method bottom, PDF p.4) — because the row-by-row polar construction is a nonlinear warp, not a similarity/affine map. The paper does not quantify this distortion (`?`).
- **More bookkeeping than a single $3\times3$ homography:** the inverse mapping back to the original images requires a per-epipolar-line lookup table of endpoint distances (§3 "Transfering information back", PDF p.4) rather than one matrix inversion — the paper frames this as still simpler than Roy et al.'s cylindrical method (which needs full 3-D operations per epipolar line), but it is more machinery than planar rectification wherever planar rectification is actually applicable.

# Numerical sensitivity

- **Arbitrary vector $a$ in $H=[e']_\times F + e'a^\top$** (Eq. 3, PDF p.2) must satisfy $\det H\neq 0$; the paper states this requirement but does not discuss how close to singular a poorly chosen $a$ can get numerically, or give a recommended default (`?`).
- **Angular step derivation is a ratio of triangle side lengths**, $\lvert bc\rvert/\lvert ac\rvert$ (Fig. 5, PDF p.3) — effectively a division whose denominator is the "far" segment length $\lvert ac\rvert$. The paper does not discuss numerical behavior as the epipole approaches the image border (where this construction becomes most active, since that is exactly the regime the classical planar method blows up in) — conceptually, near-epipole image regions require the finest angular sampling, which is precisely why the polar method subdivides finely there rather than compressing pixels or exploding image extent as the planar method would (`?` on the exact numerical stability, but the qualitative behavior is stated).
- **The height/width bounds are purely combinatorial, not data-dependent:** $2(W+H)$ and $\sqrt{W^2+H^2}$ (PDF p.4) depend only on the source image dimensions, not on where the epipole sits or how well-conditioned $F$ is — so the *bound* itself carries no numerical-conditioning risk, unlike planar rectification's output size which is directly a function of epipole proximity to the image.
- **Orientation is a discrete sign choice**, not a continuous quantity (Eq. 5, PDF p.2) — the sign-fixing step itself is not ill-conditioned in the way a continuous estimation would be, but a wrong discrete choice (from a mismatched seed correspondence) silently flips the whole image's positive/negative convention with no numerical warning signal described in the paper (`?`).

# Applicability

- **Use when:** either epipole may fall inside the image (general/forward motion, hand-held or uncalibrated structure-from-motion sequences — §1, PDF p.1–2: "New approaches in uncalibrated structure-from-motion [14] however make it possible to retrieve 3D models of scenes acquired with hand-held cameras. In this case forward motion can no longer be excluded"), and only weak calibration (an oriented fundamental matrix) is available — no metric calibration or projection matrices required. Also applicable, though "overkill" in the classical-stereo case, when epipoles are far outside the image, since the method degrades gracefully rather than assuming the epipole-outside-image case as a precondition.
- **Don't use when:** the application needs a Euclidean-looking, rectangular rectified image — for classical stereo rigs where "the main component of camera displacement is parallel to the images... the limited vergence keeps the epipoles far from the images" (§1, PDF p.1–2), the simpler planar/homography rectification produces an undistorted rectangular result and remains the more natural choice; the polar method's output is intentionally non-rectangular and visually warped in exchange for its size and generality guarantees.
- **Compared against:**
  - **Standard/traditional planar (homography-based) rectification** [1, 5, 13 in the paper's own numbering — Ayache & Hansen 1988, Faugeras's 1993 book, Papadimitriou & Dennis 1996; none registered in `docs/papers/index.yaml`] — fails outright (unboundedly large output) when an epipole is inside either image, and can still produce very large output even when the epipole is merely close to the image (§1, PDF p.1).
  - **Roy, Meunier & Cox 1997, cylindrical rectification** [ref 15 in the paper's bibliography; **not registered** in `docs/papers/index.yaml`, so it cannot be cited as a `relations[]` target — flagged here in prose only] — handles in-image epipoles and avoids pixel compression, like this paper, but at materially higher cost and without this paper's size-minimality guarantee: "The method is however relatively complex since all operations are performed in 3-dimensional space. Every epipolar line is consecutively transformed, rotated and scaled... it still assumes oriented cameras. This is not guaranteed for a weakly calibrated image pair. The main disadvantage of this method is that it does not take advantage of the fact that the matching ambiguity in stereo matching is reduced to half epipolar lines. With the epipoles in the images this leads to an unnecessary computational overhead. For some cases this could even lead to the failure of stereo algorithms that enforce the ordering constraint. Although the size of the images is always finite, it is not minimal since the diameter of the cylinder is determined on the worst case pixel for the whole image" (§1, PDF p.1). This paper's own approach is explicitly framed against this critique: simpler (all operations stay 2-D, in-image), exploits the half-epipolar-line ambiguity reduction Roy et al. ignore, and sizes each epipolar line independently rather than using one global worst-case diameter.

**Goal:**
Given only the oriented fundamental matrix between two views (no metric calibration, no known projection matrices needed), rectify both images so that corresponding points fall on corresponding rows, for **arbitrary camera motion** — including the case where either epipole lies inside the image, which classical planar-homography rectification cannot represent at all (it would require an unboundedly large output). The method guarantees zero pixel loss and bounds the output size purely from the source dimensions: height $\le 2(W+H)$, width $\le \sqrt{W^2+H^2}$.

**Algorithm:**
1. From the oriented fundamental matrix $F$ (plus one extra point correspondence beyond the $\geq 7$ used to estimate $F$, to fix the sign/orientation), derive the epipolar-line-transfer homography $H=[e']_\times F + e'a^\top$ (arbitrary $a$ with $\det H\neq 0$); use it to transfer epipolar lines between the two images, $l'\sim H^{-\top}l$.
2. Fix the orientation convention: for one known matching point pair, require the signed line-membership function $f_l(m)=l^\top m$ to agree in sign across both images; this restricts every epipolar line's ambiguity to a strictly positive scale factor and defines a consistent positive/negative half for every line in both images.
3. For each image independently, classify the epipole's position into one of 9 regions relative to the image rectangle and locate the extreme epipolar lines (through image corners); transfer the second image's extreme lines into the first via $H$ and intersect with the first image's own extreme lines to get the shared angular range to rectify.
4. Build the rectified image row by row in polar $(r,\theta)$ coordinates around the epipole: each row is one angular sector / half epipolar line; the radial length along the line is preserved unchanged (no resampling along $r$).
5. Choose the angular step between consecutive rows independently per half epipolar line: compute, via a similar-triangle construction on the worst-case pixel (always on the image border opposite the epipole), the minimum step keeping that pixel's image at least one pixel wide in the rectified row; repeat the same computation in the other image and transfer the result back; **use the minimum of the two candidate steps.**
6. If the epipole itself lies inside the image, start from an arbitrary epipolar line and sweep past 360°, overlapping by the stereo-matching-window size, to avoid a seam artifact at the wrap-around boundary.
7. Record each row's epipolar-line endpoint distances in a lookup table for inverse mapping back to the original images (with interpolation for sub-pixel accuracy); reconstruct radially (fill all pixels between two consecutive epipolar lines at once) rather than pixel-by-pixel when warping a full image back, to avoid repeated lookups.

**Implementation:**
The paper describes the construction only at the geometric/algorithmic level (Figures 3–6); no pseudocode or reference source code is published in the paper itself.

**Remarks:**
Trades a rectangular, visually undistorted rectified output (which planar rectification gives, wherever planar rectification is applicable at all) for a non-rectangular, visibly warped — but always finite, always-computable, size-bounded — output. This is a deliberate, stated trade-off, not a limitation the authors overlooked: the method is designed specifically for the regime planar rectification cannot handle (in-image epipoles, general/forward motion). See `## When to choose Pollefeys polar rectification over ...` sections, if authored, on whichever of `hartley-projective-rectification` / `loop-zhang-rectification` hosts the practitioner comparison (host determined by the more-authoritative tiebreaker at page-authoring time — do not decide it here). Also conceptually related to, but not a registered/citable comparison with, Roy, Meunier & Cox's 1997 cylindrical rectification, which solves the same in-image-epipole problem via full 3-D operations at higher complexity and without a per-line size-minimality guarantee.
